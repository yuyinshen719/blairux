Research

Who is my customer?

To have a clearer understanding of who I’m designing for, I gathered the club buyer data from merchandise analysts. Club buyers tend to be high income, single-family baby boomers, and more likely to be females (when compared to other H&D customer profiles). This customer profile data allows me to target the right audience in the next stage.

What are the current issues?

Based on the customer profile, I designed a series of usability tasks and recruited representative users from UserTesting.com. A few major issues include:

  1. Functionality Defects

    • Users thought the text description of each month was clickable while in reality it wasn’t.
    • Users tried to close the pop-up dialog by clicking elsewhere on the page.

  2. Price, length, and upsell usability problems

    • Users didn’t realize they need to select the starting month every time they change the club length.
    • Users were not sure why the page showed both a specific price as well as a price range.
    • The price was too far away from the length selection and they didn’t notice when the price changed based on the length of the subscription.
    • The upsell section was confusing as users couldn’t see the product name before selecting the checkbox.
    • The price tags were difficult to notice simply by scanning. Users didn’t notice the product description would change based on their club selection.

  3. Misunderstanding of the wording & icons

    • The upsell instruction was confusing - users often mistook it for an add-on item rather than an upgrade of the first month shipment.
    • The word “required” above the club length drop down made users think 12-month subscription was required.
    • Users were not sure how many fruits they’d get for each month. They thought the magnifying glass icon was solely for zooming in on photos while in reality it provided extra product information.

What did I find out?

Ideation

What are my goals?

  1. Find an intuitive way to present the relationship between price points and club lengths.
  2. Reduce the need to scroll up and down the page to see the product for each month.
  3. Make the upsell information more transparent and easy to understand.
  4. The page layout should work well on mobile with minimum UI change.
  5. Make the page modern and clean.

Remote user testing

Are the interactions intuitive?

Before I began testing, I made multiple variations within the prototype to test specific interactions.

Experiment 1: Do our customers react better to a touch-friendly, button UI or a traditional dropdown?

I wanted to update the design to something that felt more clean and modern while also being more mobile-friendly. It looked nice but it was a less familiar UI and I had to be sure it would work for my users. Thankfully, the initial tests showed that users appreciated the interaction.

Experiment 2: Would reflecting the selected length/date on the photo thumbnail be helpful?

To reduce scrolling for detailed information, some data was tucked away behind a click. The question for me was whether users would notice and feel comfortable with a small piece of key information regarding the length of the club purchase rather than a general information button.

A/B Testing

What brings the most business value?

Experiment 1: How can we promote the best value call out & price order?

As a promotion-focused company, our customers rely heavily on coupons and seasonal discounts. Hence highlighting those offers is essential for the product page.

After A/B testing, we learned that sorting the price range from high to low helps boost the Average Order Value. However, the red call out made some customers think the best value option is pre-selected.

I fine-tuned the best value call out to align with customer expectations by making the option pre-selected and filling the background using our branded black to distinguish from other options.

Experiment 2: How can we maximize coupon conversions?

After A/B testing for awhile, we realized customers didn’t use coupons as much on the new design. In response, I altered the coupon placement to sit above the Add To Cart button and while changing the background color of the panel to make the coupon message "pop". Conversion rate and coupon usage increased significantly after the adjustment.

What I Learned

Even a tiny visual change matters in e-commerce. B2C E-commerce has its unique UX practices where not only do we need to focus on perfect user flows but final visual details are just as critical. Of course, all of the assessments took place within a fickle market and customer behavior varied significantly from day to day. H&D’s customer demographics also lean heavily towards older adults which brings a unique set of challenges and considerations. I needed to balance business needs while advocating for the users and measuring against a fluid backdrop. It was definitely a rewarding experience and helped lay down a solid foundation for my later projects at H&D.
